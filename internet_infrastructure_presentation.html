Why the Internet
Was Built to Survive

Clark's paper captures the reasoning behind TCP/IP ‚Äî written not as a specification but as a retrospective on decisions already made. The architecture flows from a single ranked list of priorities. As Clark puts it, "an entirely different network architecture would result if the order were changed."

01

Survivability first. Communication must continue despite loss of networks or gateways. State is kept at the endpoints, not the network ‚Äî Clark's concept of "fate-sharing": it is acceptable to lose state only if the endpoint itself is also lost.

02

Multiple types of service. TCP and IP were originally one protocol; they were split precisely because different applications need different trade-offs between reliability, latency, and bandwidth. The datagram is a flexible building block, not a service in itself.

03

Accommodate a variety of networks. The only assumption: the network can transport a packet of reasonable size with imperfect reliability. This is why TCP/IP runs over Ethernet, satellite, packet radio, serial links ‚Äî and undersea fiber.

07

Accountability was last. "During wartime, one is less concerned with detailed accounting of resources used than with mustering whatever resources are available." This deprioritization haunts internet governance to this day.

Clark's Key Design Move

Why IP and TCP were separated

Originally one protocol, they were split so that IP could be the minimal, universal substrate ‚Äî while TCP added reliability only for those who needed it. UDP proves the point: sometimes you don't.

Every Packet Leaves
a Trail

Traceroute is a network diagnostic tool that maps every intermediate hop a packet makes on its journey. Each hop is a physical router, often in a specific city ‚Äî revealing exactly which real-world infrastructure your data crosses.

This tool makes Clark's abstract datagram model visible: packets truly do travel independently, hop-by-hop, through physical nodes ‚Äî the very nodes Blum photographed in his travels.

The Physical Internet
Is a Battlefield

Reuters' investigation revealed that the abstract internet has become a geopolitical flashpoint. The U.S. government's "Team Telecom" works to block any undersea cable that could connect U.S. territory to mainland China or Hong Kong, citing espionage risks.

China, meanwhile, has invested hundreds of millions in its own undersea cable infrastructure through state-owned firms and the Digital Silk Road ‚Äî a direct counterplay to Western cable dominance.

"Undersea cables have become a weapon of war in this new Cold War between China and the U.S."

‚Äî Joe Brock, Reuters reporter, 2023

Connecting the Sources

One Internet,
Four Perspectives

These four sources form a coherent argument: the internet was designed to be physical and resilient, built from glass and steel, navigable through diagnostics, and now contested as sovereign territory.

Clark ‚Üí Blum

Protocol becomes infrastructure

Clark's datagram design ‚Äî stateless gateways, endpoints bearing their own state, packets routed independently ‚Äî is exactly what Blum photographed: the fiber, the exchange points, and the landing stations executing that philosophy at planetary scale.

Blum ‚Üí Traceroute

Infrastructure becomes visible

Traceroute is a window into Blum's physical world. Each hop in a trace corresponds to a real physical node ‚Äî a router in a rack, in a building, in a city ‚Äî that Blum could walk into and touch.

Traceroute ‚Üí Reuters

Visibility reveals vulnerability

When traceroutes route through undersea cable segments, they expose the chokepoints that Reuters investigated. The millisecond gaps in latency correspond to cables that states now treat as weapons.

Reuters ‚Üí Clark

Survivability meets geopolitics

Clark ranked accountability last ‚Äî "the problem received very little attention during the early stages of the design." That gap now defines the crisis: undersea cables are owned by private consortia, governed by no single authority, and increasingly weaponized by states.

The Central Contradiction

Designed for Openness,
Contested for Control

1973
Cerf & Kahn envision TCP/IP: a protocol to interconnect any network ‚Äî designed to survive war.
1988
Clark's SIGCOMM paper: "survivability was put as a first goal, and accountability as a last goal." Retrospectively explains why TCP/IP is built the way it is ‚Äî not from specs, but from ranked military priorities.
2012
Blum's TED talk: The cloud is actually a network of buildings, copper, and fiber. The internet has a physical address.
2023
Reuters investigation: The physical internet is a geopolitical theater. The U.S. and China are waging a cold war on the ocean floor.
The core tension

Global architecture, national control

Clark built an architecture that crosses borders by design. Reuters reveals that nations are now drawing borders ‚Äî underwater ‚Äî through it.

The traceroute lesson

Every path is political

A traceroute from New York to Singapore reveals which cables your data crosses. Each of those cables is now a diplomatic negotiation, a security risk, and a potential target.

The Blum insight

Invisibility = vulnerability

Because we treat the internet as abstract, we ignore its physical stakes. Blum's lesson: when a squirrel can kill your internet, a state actor certainly can too.
